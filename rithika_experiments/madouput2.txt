--- EMPIRICIST ---
### Empirical Audit

The paper investigates the predictive and nowcasting power of Beige Book sentiment for U.S. economic activity and recessions, both nationally and regionally, using natural language processing (NLP) techniques. The core methodology involves extracting sentiment using the FinBERT model and then incorporating this sentiment into linear and logistic regression models alongside traditional macroeconomic indicators.

**Data and Preprocessing:**
*   The use of the Beige Book text from 1970 onwards is a rich and novel dataset. The application of FinBERT, a pre-trained NLP model fine-tuned on financial text, is a sound choice for sentiment extraction in this domain, aligning with current best practices in textual analysis for economic research.
*   Text cleaning steps (lowercase, stop words removal, tokenization) are standard.
*   Sentiment aggregation to quarterly (last observation) for national analysis and annual (average) for regional analysis is clearly stated.
*   The exclusion of the COVID-19 period (1980:Q2 to 2019:Q4 for main regressions) is a reasonable decision given the unique nature of that economic shock, allowing for a cleaner identification of baseline relationships. However, the paper later mentions using COVID data for out-of-sample testing, which is appropriate for evaluating robustness in extreme conditions.
*   Other macroeconomic control variables (lagged GDP growth, SPF GDP forecast, yield spread, Daily News Sentiment Index) are standard and relevant for the research questions.

**Identification Strategy and Model Specification:**
*   **National Level (GDP Growth & Recessions):**
    *   The linear regression models for nowcasting ($Y_t = \alpha + \beta_1 Y_{t-1} + \beta_2 X_t + \epsilon_t$) and forecasting ($Y_t = \alpha + \beta_1 Y_{t-1} + \beta_2 X_{t-1} + \epsilon_t$) GDP growth are standard specifications for time series analysis.
    *   Similarly, the logistic regression models for nowcasting and forecasting recessions ($Pr(Z_t = 1|Y_{t-1}, X_t)$ and $Pr(Z_t = 1|Y_{t-1}, X_{t-1})$) are appropriate for a binary dependent variable.
    *   The inclusion of lagged dependent variables helps control for autocorrelation and persistence in the economic series.
    *   The comparison of models with and without Beige Book sentiment, and against other predictors, provides a good benchmark for its incremental value.
*   **Regional Level (GDP Growth):**
    *   The panel regression models (Table 6) for regional GDP growth, including bank and year fixed effects, are well-specified to account for unobserved heterogeneity across Federal Reserve Districts and common time trends. The use of "Beige Book FinBERT sentiment (excl. Bank)" to test for spillover effects is a clever and interesting identification strategy.
*   **Topical Analysis:**
    *   The descriptive keyword searches and Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic modeling are valuable for contextualizing the textual data and are presented as complementary insights rather than formal econometric tests. The use of UMass coherence score for LDA topic selection is a recognized method.

**Statistical Validity Concerns:**
*   **Standard Errors:** A significant concern arises from the treatment of standard errors in all regression analyses. For time series regressions (Tables 1, 2, 4, 5) and panel regressions (Table 6), the paper only states "Standard errors appear in parentheses."
    *   **For time series data:** Macroeconomic time series are highly likely to exhibit serial correlation in the error terms, even after controlling for lagged dependent variables. Standard OLS/MLE standard errors would be biased and inconsistent, leading to incorrect inference (e.g., underestimated standard errors and inflated t-statistics). Robust standard errors, such as Newey-West, or explicit modeling of the error structure (e.g., AR(1) errors), are typically required.
    *   **For panel data:** The regional panel regressions (Table 6) involve observations for 12 Federal Reserve Banks over 22 years. Errors are almost certainly correlated within each bank over time. Therefore, standard errors should be clustered at the bank level at a minimum. If common shocks affect all districts in a given year, two-way clustering (by bank and by year) would be even more appropriate. Without this, the reported significance levels are likely overstated.

### Proportional Error Analysis

The paper presents a compelling case for the informational value of the Beige Book using state-of-the-art NLP techniques. The overall research design, the choice of models, and the inclusion of relevant control variables are sound. The novel contribution of regional analysis and the detailed topical insights are particularly strong.

However, the omission of a clear and appropriate method for calculating standard errors for time series and panel data is a **critical flaw**. This isn't a minor robustness check failing; it's a fundamental issue that undermines the statistical inference of *all* the quantitative results. If the standard errors are indeed underestimated due to unaddressed serial and/or cross-sectional correlation, then the reported statistical significance (p-values, asterisks) of the Beige Book sentiment, and indeed all coefficients, could be misleading. This directly impacts the claims of "statistically significant explanatory power" and "meaningful power in nowcasting and forecasting."

While the core idea and the qualitative findings (e.g., topical analysis) are valuable, the quantitative claims about significance cannot be fully trusted without this issue being addressed. The problem is pervasive across all econometric tables (Tables 1, 2, 4, 5, 6).

### Verdict

**REVISE**

The paper presents a promising and well-motivated analysis. The use of NLP on the Beige Book is timely and relevant. However, the lack of clarity and likely omission of appropriate robust or clustered standard errors for time series and panel data regressions is a fundamental econometric flaw that compromises the statistical validity of the quantitative results. This needs to be thoroughly addressed before the claims of statistical significance can be accepted. The authors should specify the type of standard errors used (e.g., heteroskedasticity and autocorrelation consistent (HAC) for time series, or clustered for panel data) and justify their choice.

--- HISTORIAN ---
**Lineage & Context**:
This paper builds upon a well-established lineage of economic literature that seeks to quantify and interpret the qualitative information contained within the Federal Reserve's Beige Book. Early predecessors, such as Balke and Petersen (2002), pioneered the conversion of Beige Book text into numerical scores to analyze GDP growth. This initial manual scoring approach was later refined by Balke, Fulmer, and Zhang (2017), who introduced algorithmic textual analysis to overcome the labor-intensive nature of manual methods. Further advancements include Sadique et al. (2013), who demonstrated the predictive power of the Beige Book's tone for economic turning points, and Gascon and Werner (2022), who utilized keyword searches to track topics like inflation and labor market trends. More recent, and notably future-dated, works like Burke and Nelson (2025) and Gascon and Martorana (2025) continue to explore the Beige Book's utility, particularly its regional information for national recession forecasting and its sentiment's correlation with business cycles.

**Gap Analysis**:
The paper presents several claims regarding its contribution to the literature:

1.  **Methodological Advancement (Sentiment Analysis)**: The authors claim to move beyond dictionary-based approaches by employing "new machine learning models," specifically FinBERT, for sentiment extraction.
    *   **Is the gap real?** Yes. Dictionary approaches are often static and lack the contextual understanding of more advanced transformer-based models like FinBERT. Applying a state-of-the-art model fine-tuned on financial text (Araci, 2019) to the Beige Book represents a genuine methodological advancement.
    *   **Do they fill it convincingly?** Yes, the paper explicitly states the use of FinBERT and describes its benefits in capturing context, which is a standard justification for such models.

2.  **Comprehensive Control Variables**: The paper aims to demonstrate the Beige Book's independent explanatory power for nowcasting and forecasting economic activity and recessions, "controlling for other relevant variables such as news sentiment from official media and the term premium, in addition to GDP forecasts by professionals."
    *   **Is the gap real?** This is less about a completely new gap and more about a rigorous and comprehensive approach to establishing incremental value. While previous studies have shown predictive capabilities, explicitly controlling for a broader set of strong, established macroeconomic predictors (e.g., yield spread, news sentiment, SPF forecasts) is a standard and necessary step to isolate the unique contribution of Beige Book sentiment. This enhances the robustness and credibility of the findings.
    *   **Do they fill it convincingly?** The econometric analysis (Tables 1, 2, 4, 5) with various control variables is a direct attempt to fill this gap, and the results, showing varying degrees of significance, address the claim.

3.  **Regional Economic Activity**: The paper states, "We also show that the Beige Book can shed light on regional economic activity as well, which the literature has not touched upon."
    *   **Is the gap real?** This claim is phrased quite strongly. While Burke and Nelson (2025) are cited as providing "evidence that Beige Book information at the individual Reserve Bank level helps forecast recessions at the national level," suggesting prior work *uses* regional Beige Book data, this paper's specific focus on explaining *regional GDP growth* and identifying *inter-regional spillover effects* is indeed a distinct contribution. The dependent variable in their regional analysis is regional economic activity, and the exploration of how sentiment from other districts influences a given district's activity is novel. So, while the literature has "touched upon" regional *data* from the Beige Book, the specific analysis of *regional outcomes* and *spillovers* appears to be a genuine, albeit perhaps not entirely "untouched," extension. The phrasing "has not touched upon" is an overstatement in its absolute sense, but the *specific nature* of their regional analysis is a real contribution.
    *   **Do they fill it convincingly?** Yes, the panel regressions (Table 6) and regional nowcasts (Figure 8) directly address this claim, showing statistically significant relationships and spillover effects.

4.  **Richer Context and Topic Evolution**: The authors propose to provide "richer context in terms of how different topics evolved throughout the past half century, including how international developments were mentioned... and how different periods of recessions differed in their substance," using keyword searches and topic modeling (LDA).
    *   **Is the gap real?** Yes. While Gascon and Werner (2022) used keyword lists for specific topics, this paper aims for a broader, more nuanced historical contextualization using LDA, which is a more sophisticated approach to topic extraction than simple keyword counts. Understanding the "substance" of different recessionary periods through topic modeling offers a valuable qualitative insight.
    *   **Do they fill it convincingly?** Yes, the topical analysis (Figures 9-12, Table 7) demonstrates the ability to track specific issues (supply chain, inflation, unemployment, international affairs) and characterize different recessionary periods, providing the "richer context" promised.

--- EMPIRICIST ---
**Empirical Audit**

The paper employs appropriate Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques, specifically FinBERT for sentiment analysis and Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) for topic modeling, which are state-of-the-art for textual data, particularly financial text. The empirical strategy utilizes standard econometric models: linear regressions for nowcasting and forecasting GDP growth, and logistic regressions for nowcasting and forecasting recessions at the national level. The inclusion of a comprehensive set of control variables (lagged GDP, yield spread, news sentiment, and SPF forecasts) is commendable for isolating the incremental value of Beige Book sentiment. The regional analysis uses panel regressions with fixed effects, which is an appropriate approach for controlling for unobserved heterogeneity across Federal Reserve Banks and over time. The decision to exclude the COVID-19 period from the main estimation sample (1980:Q2 to 2019:Q4 for national, 2002-2023 for regional) is reasonable given its unique nature, with subsequent out-of-sample tests providing some validation for the COVID period.

However, a critical econometric weakness lies in the reporting of standard errors. For all regression tables (Tables 1, 2, 4, 5, 6), the paper merely states, "Standard errors appear in parentheses," without specifying whether these are robust to heteroskedasticity, or more importantly, clustered for autocorrelation in time series data or for within-panel correlation in the regional panel data. In time series analysis, autocorrelation is a common feature, necessitating Heteroskedasticity and Autocorrelation Consistent (HAC) standard errors. In panel data, observations within the same entity (Reserve Bank) are typically correlated over time, requiring clustering of standard errors at the entity level. Without these corrections, the reported p-values and statistical significance levels are likely biased, potentially leading to an overstatement of the precision and significance of the estimated coefficients. Furthermore, while the paper discusses the statistical significance of logistic regression coefficients, it does not present marginal effects or odds ratios, which are more interpretable for non-linear models. Finally, the regional analysis uses annual data, contrasting with the quarterly frequency of the national analysis, which could limit direct comparability of dynamic effects, though this might be driven by data availability.

**Proportional Error Analysis**

The most significant statistical flaw is the unspecified nature of the standard errors. If the standard errors are not robustly estimated (e.g., HAC for time series, clustered for panel data), then the reported statistical significance (p-values) of the coefficients is unreliable. This is a fundamental issue that could invalidate many of the paper's core claims regarding "statistically significant explanatory power" and "robust performance." This flaw is potentially *fatal* to the empirical validity of the findings as currently presented, as it directly impacts the confidence one can place in the hypothesis tests.

Other flaws, such as the acknowledged "minimal evidence of contributing to out-of-sample forecasting performance" for GDP growth (Table 3), are less severe because the authors transparently report this limitation, and the recession forecasting performance appears stronger. The lack of marginal effects for logistic regressions is a matter of interpretability rather than validity, and the use of annual data for regional analysis, while a point of discussion, is likely a practical constraint and doesn't inherently invalidate the findings, provided the standard errors are correctly handled. However, the standard error issue casts a shadow over all statistical inferences, making it the primary concern.

This paper builds upon a well-established tradition of leveraging the Federal Reserve's Beige Book for economic insights, moving beyond its initial role as a confidential policy document (Red Book, 1970-1983) to a publicly accessible source of "soft information." Predecessors include Balke and Petersen (2002) and Balke, Fulmer, and Zhang (2017), who pioneered converting Beige Book text into quantitative scores for GDP analysis, initially through manual scoring and later with early algorithmic methods. More recent work by Sadique et al. (2013), Gascon and Werner (2022), Burke and Nelson (2025), and Gascon and Martorana (2025) further explored the Beige Book's predictive capabilities for economic activity, inflation, labor markets, and recessions, often employing dictionary-based approaches or basic textual analysis. The current paper advances this lineage by incorporating state-of-the-art Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques, specifically the FinBERT model (Araci, 2019), and by expanding the scope to regional economic activity and a more granular, historically contextualized topical analysis, drawing on methods like Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) and keyword searches. It also situates its findings against established macroeconomic predictors such as the yield spread (Haubrich, 2006), news sentiment indices (Shapiro, Sudhof and Wilson, 2022), and professional forecasts (Eo and Morley, 2023; Engelberg, Manski and Williams, 2009).

The paper claims to fill several gaps:
1.  **Application of new machine learning models**: "In this paper, we provide a general overview of how sentiment measured by new machine learning models can help nowcast and predict economic activity and recessions..." 2.  **Regional economic activity analysis**: "We also show that the Beige Book can shed light on regional economic activity as well, which the literature has not touched upon." (p. 5). A concrete example where a statistically significant coefficient is particularly susceptible to becoming insignificant if appropriate robust/clustered standard errors were applied is the "Beige Book FinBERT sentiment (Bank-level)" coefficient in Table 6, Model (1). This coefficient is reported as 1.053** with a standard error of 0.409. The current t-statistic is approximately 1.053 / 0.409 ≈ 2.57, which is significant at the 5% level (p<0.05). However, in panel data regressions with fixed effects, standard errors are often clustered at the entity level (e.g., Federal Reserve Bank) to account for within-panel correlation over time. If clustering were applied, it is common for standard errors to increase, sometimes substantially. For instance, if the clustered standard error were to increase to 0.55 (a plausible increase, roughly 35% higher than reported), the t-statistic would drop to 1.053 / 0.55 ≈ 1.91. This would render the coefficient statistically insignificant at the conventional 5% level (which typically requires a t-statistic of at least 1.96 for a large sample).

The policy implication of such a change is significant. The paper claims that "Reserve Bank-level Beige Book sentiment significantly explains variation in regional GDP growth" (p. 31).
